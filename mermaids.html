Appearance

Mermaids are usually portrayed as creatures with the head and torso of a woman (or person in general, but women are more commonly portrayed in the media), and the tail of a fish. They are depicted as beautiful creatures, but they can be good or bad, resembling humans in this sense. Some can fall in love with humans, and at times they can bestow good things upon people, but when they are upset, they can bring forth harm, such as in the form of shipwrecks. Due to this, seeing a mermaid while at sea was seen as bad luck in the past.

Portrayal in Literature

The Little Mermaid

The Original Version

The book “The Little Mermaid” by Hans Christian Anderson was published in 1837. The story is a romance and a tragedy, but one with a happy ending, and it is about a mermaid who longs to be part of the human world, and falls in love with a prince who she saves from a shipwreck. However, the prince never knows this, and he falls in love with someone else, who he thought was his saviour. The Little Mermaid longs even more to be human after meeting the prince, so she goes to the Sea Witch, who gives her a potion to give her legs in exchange for her beautiful voice. Once on the shore, the Little Mermaid is found by the prince, and he takes her in, though does not fall in love with her like she had originally wanted. Instead, he wishes to marry the other woman, who he thought was the one who saved his life. On the day of their wedding, the Little Mermaid’s sisters come to her and give her a dagger. They tell her that in exchange for their hair, they made a deal with the Sea Witch that if the Little Mermaid killed the prince, she would become a mermaid again. However, the Little Mermaid cannot bring herself to kill the prince, and instead throws herself and the dagger into the sea. Although she expected to dissolve into foam, like all mermaids do when they die, she becomes a “Daughter of the Air”, or a spirit, because of her selflessness.

Disney’s Adaptation

In 1989, Disney published an animated movie named “The Little Mermaid” that was based off of Hans Christian Anderson’s novel. The movie follows Ariel, a sixteen year old mermaid who longs to be human and immerse herself in the human world. The general plot is very similar to that of the original novel’s, however some aspects of it have been changed to make it (arguably) more appealing and child-friendly. The biggest differences are the ending and the other woman who the prince marries, as the in the Disney version, the Sea Witch disguises herself as a beautiful woman to sabotage Ariel and take the prince away from her, but in the end, Ariel stays human and marries the prince.

Ariel from “The Little Mermaid”.

The official reason for the differences between the book and the movie of this story is unknown, but certain factors may have played a part in the changes. For example, many of Disney’s animated movies are for the entire family, so they may have deemed the scene in the book where the Little Mermaid’s sisters tell her to kill the prince to be a bit too dark for some members of their audience. Additionally, there are certain tropes in Disney movies, such as the protagonist finding love in and getting married to the prince (or any character), so the fact that the Little Mermaid doesn’t do this, and the prince ends up marrying someone else, might have been too different for Disney. In the book, there were also a few allusions to religion, so to appeal to a greater audience, Disney may have chosen to stay away from this theme.

Similar Mythical Creatures

Sirens

Sirens are often used interchangeably with mermaids, however they were not thought to be the same being in the early days of their existence. Sirens used to be depicted as half-bird and half-human, but by the middle ages, this description had changed so that sirens were half-fish and half-human. In other words, their appearance became that of mermaids. Like mermaids, sirens are depicted as being very beautiful, however in the myths surrounding them, mainly in Greek Mythology, it was said that they would lure sailors to the rocks with their enchanting voices, causing the boats to crash. The most famous example of a work of literature with this occurrence is “The Odyssey” by Homer.
